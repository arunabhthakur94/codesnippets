How most companies get Diversity Recruiting completely, embarrassingly wrong

Ciara Trinidad
Follow
May 6, 2016 · 5 min read

I’m what you might call a triple threat: I’m a woman, black, and identify as queer.

As you can imagine, I was a favorite target of middle school bullies who found a lot of reasons to not like me. But while I spent my childhood constantly explaining who I am, how I identify, and where my ancestors came from, the past 7 years have been a stark contrast. Being in a city as diverse as San Francisco and working in tech, I’ve been incredibly lucky to be a part of inclusive companies where I’ve felt accepted and empowered. But I’m acutely aware that not everyone has had the same opportunities. That’s why I’m so passionate about helping other companies build and emulate the inclusive environments that I’ve experienced first hand.

I love my job in Customer Success at Lever, enterprise hiring software. In addition to an amazing product, a passionate team, and a mission I’m proud of, I work with innovative companies all over Silicon Valley and beyond. Partnering with amazing recruiting teams at companies like Netflix, Medium, Lyft, etc. has given me a unique vantage point. My work allows me to observe and learn how different companies think about HR, in both acquiring and developing employees in this intense market for top talent.

One trend that’s sweeping the Valley is Diversity & Inclusion, which became mainstream when the tech giants decided to release their diversity data a couple of years ago . The move sparked an important conversation about the underrepresentation of women and minorities in certain functions, and often, across entire companies.

Eager, ambitious recruiters are rising to the seemingly impossible challenge posed by their teams: Get more diversity in here. Where are the black people? Why aren’t there more women? Fix this. The best recruiters I know absolutely crush it because 1. they’re strategic and 2. they genuinely love helping people find jobs. Diversity Recruiting is not only a competitive tactic, it also means helping underrepresented communities find jobs that they deserve at companies that overlooked them previously. Still, because there aren’t any “best practices” for diversity recruiting yet, we’re all breaking new ground. That means we’re all making potential missteps and trying things just to see if they work.

That being said, the tone of emails I receive from recruiters has changed in the last couple of years. Reachouts that included a D&I mention used to be refreshing, but now everyone is doing it. I find myself over-analyzing messages, trying to figure out if it’s my skills and qualifications they want, or my appearance. I can only imagine how much worse female engineers have it.

Diversity is hot right now, and when I receive recruiting messages that tout that buzzword, my inner skeptic whispers, “ the only reason you say you care is because everyone else is saying it too …” You could ask if intentions matter as long as the end result is greater diversity — I’d say so. I am merely observing more people jumping in because they have diversity stats to pad, not because they’re actually invested in creating more inclusive workplaces for diverse talent. You can’t blame us for becoming more wary.

That’s why it infuriates me when I see articles like “4 Simple Ways to Increase Your Company’s Diversity” written by white men. Because what it really does is paint diversity as a “problem,” one that can be fixed “simply.” It might seem simple, because unless you’ve tried really, really hard to understand the experience of someone like me, it’s natural to assume D&I can be boiled down to a few “quick and easy” fixes. This line of thinking, even if not meant to be harmful, reveals a lack of awareness. It trivializes my experience as a human being and all of the challenges I’ve had to overcome as a minority. What we’re talking about are deeply complex human issues, and thus, won’t be solved by a few “simple” tips any time soon.

So here’s my request to all HR professionals and recruiters interested in D&I: don’t use me as a way to check the diversity box. In your Linkedin reachouts, it’s not enough to mention that your company has a “focus on diversity.” The proof is in the pudding: is your company actually open and welcoming to all kinds of people? If not, start there. Establish ERGs (Employee Resource Groups), start a book club, support underrepresented groups to sponsor and host events. Lead workshops about unconscious bias and create forums for honest conversation and mutual learning. Allow and train different types of employees to get involved in your hiring process, and evaluate ways to minimize interviewer bias.

If your company is already an incredible place to work, awesome . Then let’s dig into the D&I culture — what have you *done*? Send me photos from an event, share stories from your employees, or talks that illustrate your philosophy on diversity. Connect me to folks who can help me better understand what working at your company feels like. Show, not tell, that you’re invested in building a safe and inclusive workplace for people like me. And if you aren’t there yet and all of this sounds new and scary, no worries at all — you’re in the perfect position to get started. There are plenty of resources out there designed to help you, like the newly-introduced Project Include . You just have to choose to go seek them out!

So hell yeah, I am a triple threat: I’m passionate about customer experiences, I’ve helped build incredible teams from scratch, and I’m constantly pushing myself and those around me towards ambitious goals. This is what sets me apart, what makes me a great candidate, one you’d be stoked to have on your team. And showing that you value true diversity and inclusion would make me stoked to be on yours.
