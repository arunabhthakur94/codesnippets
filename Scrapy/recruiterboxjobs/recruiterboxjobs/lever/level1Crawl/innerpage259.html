Meet Graham Pionkowski, Lever’s Director of Customer Advocacy

Lever
Follow
Aug 15, 2018 · 10 min read

Graham has a lot of empathy for Lever’s customers — because he used to be one. He was introduced to Lever when he implemented the software at his previous company in Austin. Along the way, Graham got to know the team at Lever well, he even spoke at our Talent Innovation Summit. When he was ready for his next challenge, Lever was top of mind, and the rest is history.

Graham came on board to take Lever’s customer-centric philosophy to the next level: to create an entire team that’s charter is to drive product understanding, adoption, and alignment in order to make sure our customers are successful. We talked to Graham to learn more about his growing team.

Let’s start with the basics, tell me about the Customer Advocacy team.

Our primary mission is making sure our customers are getting the most value possible from our products. That means we need to understand their business goals, recruitment strategies, and internal operation needs so we can act as true consultants. Every year, our customers’ strategies and hiring needs transform — we want to be part of that transformation by helping to guide and support them.

My team listens to our customer’s needs and keeps an open dialogue with them so they’re in the loop on what we’re doing to support their product and services needs. This includes our current solutions as well as our future roadmap.

Every account has different needs and we work to provide a customer journey that works for them. We manage the relationship proactively and holistically so that each customer has a top-notch experience with Lever and our team. Our CSM support is a competitive advantage for them to leverage as they build their teams.

Customer Advocacy is Lever’s newest team. Talk to me about that.

Yep, we’re not even a year old. Which makes for a great opportunity — there’s no playbook to follow, we’re writing the playbook. And we’re already hearing from customers that our expertise and guidance has helped them become much more efficient and effective.

Lever has always had a best-in-class product, and we have put a huge emphasis on the R&D side of the business. But we know the software is only as good as its setup and the alignment with the customer’s unique organization and their internal processes. Lever is really investing in our customer’s success, and that means bringing on teams who are entirely focused on them, and their unique needs.

We’re also using our customers’ feedback to chart the vision for the future of Lever so their input and product feedback is directly contributing to our product and their their success using it for years to come.

“Our ability to influence the product roadmap is a huge differentiator and shows we’re walking the walk, not just talking the talk. We’re called Customer Advocacy for a reason.”

That sets this team apart from teams of Customer Success Managers at other companies, right?

Definitely. At Lever, we have direct lines of communication to every single department, especially to Product. This helps us influence innovation and change in a major way — which really matters to customers. They want to see their feedback reflected in how we build the product. Our ability to influence the product roadmap is a huge differentiator and shows we’re walking the walk, not just talking the talk. We’re called Customer Advocacy for a reason.

What’s also pretty different about our team is that we have no quota. When you don’t have a quota, you don’t have to worry about trying to sell the customer something they don’t need or prioritizing your goals over theirs: all you’re focusing on is making them successful. Our customers rely on us, trust us, seek advice from us, and know we’re there for them. We’re truly consultative without any other business agenda. That’s actually one of the reasons I chose Lever as a former customer.

On that note, let’s back up. How did you get into the world of Recruiting and eventually Lever?

Well, before Lever I was leading talent acquisition for one of Lever’s current customers. I managed a team, crafted a global strategy, and got hands on with the technology to make sure we could support the growth of the business.

I’ve actually been in recruiting for the majority of my career. When I graduated from grad school, I stumbled into doing recruiting operations. This gave me my first exposure to recruiting technologies, business process engineering, and marrying systems with policies and procedure, and so on.

I then ended up in the front lines as a recruiter. I was a corporate recruiter and focused on sales, G&A, and even some technical recruiting. With that first hand experience in talent acquisition, I feel like I understand the entire scope of the function, from the front-end to the behind the scenes, well. I really understand and empathize with our customer’s goals and challenges.

What brought you to Lever?

I had a great relationship with Phil, our implementation specialist while I was at BazaarVoice. We got a lot done and developed a very trusting and tight relationship. I also had gotten to know Todd (Lever’s SVP of Customer Success) and instantly felt a connection with him as well.

I was ready to do something new, and wanted to focus on recruiting technology, and thought maybe product management was the answer. I was actually the one who pursued Lever. I put myself on Todd’s radar and said, “I don’t know where I fit in, but I like what you’re doing over there.”

Todd asked if I’d be interested in this type of role, I’d never thought about it before, but it really seemed like the best mix of all worlds. And truly, it is. I’m at the center of all the things that really get me excited and I’m passionate about.

I get to build a team and act as a coach for them. I get to work directly with customers and have really interesting conversations about their talent acquisition challenges. I get to work hands-on with the product. I get to work with the product and engineering team to help plot the vision for what’s next. At night while I’m wrapping up, I’ll get pulled over to do user testing to give feedback around a new feature. It’s a ton of fun, I practically beg the design team to loop me into user testing. They’re not sick of me yet.

You actually moved to San Francisco for Lever?

I did, I uprooted everything, packed up and left Austin right away, motorcycle in tow. I thought I could make a really big impact on something bigger than just one organization’s mission to hire the right people… and went all in. I felt a really deep connection with Lever’s product, leadership and culture (and of course the product opportunities) and wanted to be a part of something that I could just sense was a game changer.

I moved to Lever, I didn’t move to San Francisco. That’s the way I look at it.

How was being a customer first shaped your team’s ethos?

I’ve worked with a lot of account managers and customer success teams throughout my career. Many were subject matter experts on their specific product, willing to take product feedback and work with me to align on contracts, but the major piece that was missing for me was that consultative approach. That ability to understand talent acquisition holistically, where the industry was going, and serve as true partner and also a talent acquisition consultant. They didn’t push me to think differently or help me problem solve.

My aim is to create a differentiator here at Lever with both the people we hire on the team as well as how we’re organized to serve our customers. All of our customers have unique challenges, so we’re hiring and training subject matter experts and true consultants to help them problem solve and discovery opportunities to better their abilities to attract and hire. They’re not just getting someone who understands our products. They’re not just getting somebody who will listen to their product feedback and bring that to our product team. They’re actually here to help the customer problem solve, do that in a consultative way, understanding the landscape of recruiting and all its complexities.

It’s not just doing the business fundamentals of what a customer success team would normally do in our space, but going above and beyond that.

“Instead of getting your MBA, come join my team. Seriously. It’s a lot cheaper, and you’ll learn more in six months than at a top business school.”

From your very biased opinion, why is your team the best one in the company to work on?

First, our current team, and we’re just at about eight now, are some of the funniest, kindest, most engaged folks I’ve ever had the pleasure of working with. On the team, as small as it is, we have motorcycle fans, urban-chicken raisers, podcast producers, drag stars, soccer super-fans, a remote team member in Chicago, and a person who left Lever to travel the world for a year and then returned. It’s a diverse team, and we’re incredibly tight-knit and have a ton of fun working together.

That said, what unites us even more is that we’re passionate about the product and making sure it’s contributing to our customers’ success.We have the opportunity to work with our customers, of course, but we’re also working with every team inside Lever. So, if you join Customer Advocacy, there won’t be a face or a name that you won’t know. We work with Sales. We work with Customer Success. We work with Engineering. We work with Product. It’s an amazing opportunity to build connections and get a 360 understanding of the business. Instead of getting your MBA, come join my team. Seriously. It’s a lot cheaper, and you’ll learn more in six months than at a top business school.

“I’m looking for individuals with a builder mindset, who want to be a part of something from the ground floor.”

You have a pretty big mix of people from the business on your team. Where did the people come from? Whether they were inside Lever or coming externally.

Yeah, we have pretty diverse backgrounds. Some of them, like Kika and Chelsea, were very early Lever employees, some were working with customers on other teams at Lever, some came from consultant-type roles, and some joined us from other top HR tech companies.

Your attitude and aptitude are more important than your background, honestly. At the end of the day, I’m looking for people with a growth mindset, who want to be the best at what they do.

I’m also looking for individuals with a builder mindset, who want to be a part of something from the ground floor. Our team is laying a foundation for the future of the organization three, four, five, ten years from now. The work that we do now, the tone that we set, the differentiation that we set amongst ourselves and our competitors and those relationships that we create with our customers, is really setting a bar for where we’re going to want to continue to leap over year over year. So a builder mindset, a growth mindset, the willingness to “play in the white space” while we continue to build the function are key. There’s a lot of challenges in being in such a new function but it’s also lots of fun.

Who wouldn’t necessarily do so well on your team?

People looking to join an organization where you have a happy path from A to Z — who want a rigid working environment that isn’t as fast-paced. Someone who doesn’t want to truly get out of the building or get out of their desk and go interact with others in the business also wouldn’t succeed. It’s crucial for us to go to lunch and sit with a group of team members from across the organization, get out of our seats to go talk to people face to face instead of sending a Slack message or email. Bottom line: we have to work really well cross-functionally. A healthy sense of humor is also a plus.

I like to refer to Advocacy as a startup within a startup. Other teams and other functions have been around for a long time and are miles and miles ahead of us process-wise. But we have the opportunity to innovate within our own space and think about how to solve our own problems as well as our customer problems, just a little bit differently. I think that opportunity to pave the way and do something new is pretty exciting.
